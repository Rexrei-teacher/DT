IGCSE 0445 Design & Technology: India's Food Security Challenge

Welcome to your GA3 assessment. The focus of this project is to research, design, and propose a viable solution to a specific problem related to food security in India. This webpage will guide you through the three key components of your submission: developing a solution, creating a design brief, and conducting a peer review.

1. Reasoning a Solution Based on Needs

In IGCSE Design & Technology, a successful solution is not just a "good idea"; it is a direct response to a clearly identified need. Your entire project must be driven by this principle.

How to Reason from Needs:

  1. Identify the Problem & User: You can't solve "India's food problem"‚Äîit's too big. You must narrow it down. Who is your specific user (e.g., a small-hold farmer, a street vendor, a family in a rural village)? What is their *specific* problem (e.g., "30% of tomato harvest spoils before reaching the market due to heat and poor transport")?
  2. Analyse the Needs: A "need" is the gap between the current problem and the desired outcome.
    • Problem: Crops are spoiling from heat.
    • Need: A low-cost, non-electric method to keep vegetables cool.
    • Problem: Inefficient water use for irrigation during droughts.
    • Need: A system to deliver water directly to plant roots with minimal waste.
  3. Generate Ideas (Divergent Thinking): Brainstorm multiple possible solutions that address the specific need. Don't pick your first idea! Think about different approaches (e.g., for cooling: a pot-in-pot cooler, a solar-powered fan, a ventilated storage crate).
  4. Select and Justify (Convergent Thinking): Evaluate your ideas against the user's needs and constraints (like cost, materials, local skills). Select the *most appropriate* solution and justify *why* it is the best choice for this specific user and problem. Your solution must be a logical and defensible answer to the need.

2. The Design Brief

What is a Design Brief?

The Design Brief is the most important document in your project. It is a clear statement that defines the problem you are going to solve, who you are solving it for, and the rules (constraints) you must follow. It acts as a "contract" for your design, guiding all your decisions and helping you measure your success.

How to Construct a Design Brief:

A good brief contains two main parts: a clear problem statement and a list of specifications (constraints).

1. Problem Statement

This is a short paragraph that sets the scene.

  • Who: Who is the target user/client?
  • What: What is their problem?
  • Where: What is the context/environment?
  • Why: Why does this problem need solving?
2. Design Specifications (Constraints)

This is a bulleted list of measurable rules your design MUST follow.

  • Function: What must it do? (e.g., "Must be able to store 10kg of produce.")
  • Cost: Max/min budget? (e.g., "Must cost less than ‚Çπ500 to build.")
  • Materials: What can it be made from? (e.g., "Must use locally available materials like clay, sand, or bamboo.")
  • Aesthetics: What should it look like? (e.g., "Must be unobtrusive and easy to clean.")
  • Safety: How will it be safe? (e.g., "Must have no sharp edges.")
  • Size/Weight: (e.g., "Must be portable by one person.")

Example Design Brief:

Problem Statement:

"Small-hold farmers in rural Maharashtra, India, often lose 25-40% of their fruit and vegetable harvest to spoilage before they can sell it at the local market. This is primarily due to high temperatures and a lack of affordable, reliable cold storage, as electricity access is intermittent. A low-cost, non-electric cooling device is needed to extend the shelf life of their produce, allowing them to sell more and increase their income."

Design Specifications:

  • The device must require no electricity to operate.
  • The total cost of materials must not exceed ‚Çπ800 (approx. $10 USD).
  • It must be buildable using tools commonly available in a rural village.
  • It must use locally sourced materials (e.g., clay, sand, jute, bamboo).
  • It must be able to lower the internal temperature by at least 8¬∞C compared to the ambient temperature.
  • It must have a minimum capacity to hold 15kg of produce.
  • The device must be durable and last at least one year with minimal maintenance.

3. Sketching & Ideation

Sketching is how designers think and communicate. In IGCSE D&T, your sketches are not marked on artistic ability, but on their clarity and ability to communicate your ideas. You must show your thinking process.

Key Sketching Tips:

Communicate, Don't Decorate
  • Clarity over Beauty: Use simple lines. A clear, well-labelled diagram is better than a "pretty" but confusing drawing.
  • Use 2D and 3D: Use quick 2D (orthographic) sketches to show dimensions and 3D (isometric, perspective) sketches to show what the product will look like.
  • Show Scale: Give a sense of size. You can draw a common object (like a hand or a brick) next to your design for scale, or add dimensions.
Annotation is Essential
  • Label Everything: Use leader lines to point to parts of your design.
  • Explain "What": Label materials (e.g., "bamboo pole," "terracotta pot," "recycled plastic sheet").
  • Explain "How": Label how parts connect (e.g., "bolted joint," "welded," "friction fit").
  • Explain "Why": Justify your choices (e.g., "vents for airflow," "rounded edges for safety").

What to Include:

  • Initial Ideas (Brainstorm): Show lots of different, quick sketches to explore multiple solutions. It's okay if they are rough.
  • Development Sketches: Take your best idea and draw it again, adding details, solving problems (like "how will the lid attach?"), and refining the design.
  • Exploded Views: Show how the components fit together by "exploding" them apart.
  • Sectional Views: Show a "slice" through your design to reveal internal parts and how they work.

4. Peer Review Process

Peer review is a critical part of the design process. It helps you identify strengths and weaknesses in your project that you might have missed. Your task is to review the Design Brief and Initial Solution Concept of one of your classmates.

Use the "Two Stars and a Wish" model: two things that are done well (Stars) and one specific suggestion for improvement (a Wish). Be constructive and specific.

Review Checklist:

When reviewing your peer's work, consider the following questions.

Reviewing the Design Brief:
  • Is the problem statement clear and specific? Do you understand exactly who the user is and what their problem is?
  • Are the design specifications measurable? (e.g., "Must be cheap" is bad. "Must cost less than ‚Çπ500" is good).
  • Are the constraints realistic for the context of rural India?
  • Does the brief clearly define what a "successful" solution would look like?
Reviewing the Solution Concept:
  • Does the proposed solution directly solve the problem stated in the design brief?
  • Does the solution meet all of the design specifications (constraints) listed?
  • Is the solution appropriate for the target user? (e.g., Is it too complex? Too expensive?)
  • Is there clear evidence of research into the problem (e.g., research on climate, local materials, existing solutions)?
